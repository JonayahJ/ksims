The Research

Intersectional Discrimination and Change in Blood Pressure Control among Older Adults

Takeaways

This work filled evidential gaps by evaluating whether perceived discrimination is associated with developing hypertension or prevents return to a normotensive state. We also assessed whether the effects are compounded among people reporting intersectional discrimination (e.g., both classism and ageism). Additionally we explored gender differences in blood pressure control attributable to biased treatment.

(1) Experiences of lifetime as well as intersectional everyday discrimination may elevate hypertensive risk among older men.

(2) These patterns were not observed among similarly aged women.

(3) Discrimination is associated with a lower likelihood of antihypertensive medication use among both genders.
